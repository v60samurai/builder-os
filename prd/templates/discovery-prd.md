# How to write this

Read this page once before you start, and once more before you send the paper
out. It is the only part of this pack that is about writing rather than thinking.

## Write the way you would explain it to a friend

Your reader has not met your people and has not read your notes. Everything they
believe about your work, they will believe because of the way you put it on the
page. A hard idea in plain words reads as confident. A simple idea in long words
reads as someone hiding.

| Not this | This instead |
|---|---|
| We leveraged qualitative methods to surface latent needs | I spoke to nine people and asked what they did last time |
| Friction is disproportionately concentrated at this juncture | Most of the pain sits on one step |
| Users articulated a preference for enhanced visibility | People said they wanted to know where their order was |

Cut on sight: leverage, ecosystem, utilise, facilitate, robust, seamless,
holistic, granular, actionable, learnings, moving the needle. The test is whether
you would say the sentence out loud to a person.

## Full sentences, and no dashes

A fragment feels sharp when you write it and reads as choppy to everyone else,
because they have to put the meaning back together. Write full sentences. Use a
comma or a full stop where you were reaching for a long dash. Once you use one
dash every sentence gets one, and the page turns into a chain of interruptions.

## Tell the story of working it out

A report lists what you found. A story shows how you came to understand it, so
your reader reaches your conclusion having followed the same road. Same facts,
different order, and the second one is far harder to argue with.

| A report | A story |
|---|---|
| Finding one appeared in nine of twelve. Finding two in eight of twelve. Both relate to reliability. | The strongest pattern was about time, and it showed up in nine of twelve. What made it more than an annoyance was what people did about it. Seven of them had built a workaround they never mentioned until I asked. |

## The same finding, written twice

This is one finding from one imaginary study, written first the way most first
drafts arrive, then the way it should read. Nothing about the content changed.
Only the order and the words did.

| First draft | Second draft |
|---|---|
| It was observed that a significant proportion of respondents leveraged informal workarounds. 7/12 (58%) of participants indicated they utilise manual tracking methods. This suggests a potential opportunity around visibility tooling. | Seven of the twelve people I met keep a second, private list. Nobody mentioned it until I asked, because none of them think of it as a system. One woman keeps hers on the back of a delivery slip and rewrites it every Monday morning. That is forty minutes a week, spent working around a gap nobody has ever reported. |

The second one is longer and it reads faster. Every sentence adds a fact instead
of softening one. It also cannot be confused with anybody else's paper.

## Six habits that cost you a reader

| The habit | The fix |
|---|---|
| Writing to sound like a PM | Use the plain word. The best papers read like a clear person explaining something they understand. |
| Hedging every sentence | Say what you found. Where you are unsure, use the tag. That is what tags are for. |
| Burying the point at the end of a paragraph | Put it in the first sentence. Readers skim, and the first sentence always gets read. |
| Explaining your method instead of your finding | "Nine of twelve described the same thing" beats "I ran a thematic analysis." Method belongs in one place only. |
| Numbers with nothing to compare them to | "Scored 20, the highest on the map and double the next one" gives the number a job. |
| Hiding who decided | "I chose" beats "it was determined that." |

> **Two tests that catch the most.** Read the whole thing out loud, which takes
> about twenty minutes. You will hear the fragments, the hedging, and the
> sentences that say nothing. Then hand it to somebody who has never heard of
> your case. Ask them what the problem is, and what you decided. If they cannot
> tell you, the fault is not theirs.

# Discovery PRD

One problem, worked from the field notes up.

## How this template works

This template gives you the structure of a discovery paper and none of its
answers. Each section is built around one hard question, and you answer it from
your own field notes. The questions show you where to dig. They never show you
what to find.

Anyone can fill in this same template, and no two papers should sound alike. If
your paper could trade places with somebody else's, you have not done discovery
yet.

Every table here is a floor and not a ceiling. Add rows when your notes need
room, and cut rows you do not use. Write plain, in full sentences, using the
words people actually spoke to you.

## The page budget

Set a page limit before you start writing, and spend it in advance. That way the
hard cuts happen at the start instead of in a panic at the end. The budget below
assumes fifteen pages. Adjust the numbers if your limit is different, but keep
the ratio: roughly three quarters of the paper belongs to the problem. Do not
steal problem pages to fatten the solution.

> **This template is longer than the paper you will write, and that is fine.**
> The extra length is guidance, empty rows, and writing space. None of it counts
> toward your limit. Delete every note as you use it. Cut the rows you do not
> fill. The paper that is left is the one that gets read.

| Part | Sections | Pages | Weight |
|---|---|---|---|
| A · Working out what is going on | 1 to 6 | 5.5 | 40% |
| B · Finding the gaps | 7 to 11 | 5.5 | 40% |
| C · What I would try | 12 to 13 | 2.5 | 20% |
| Front page and self-check | | 1.5 | |

Each section states its own budget under the title. Treat that budget as a
constraint you agreed to, not a suggestion.

## Evidence tags

Every claim your case leans on gets a tag, typed right after the claim, like
this: **(E2, 6 of 9)**. The number ranks the strength of the proof, and lower is
stronger.

| Tag | What it means | Example of how you got it |
|---|---|---|
| **E1** | You watched it happen, counted it, or pulled it from a record | Sat with someone while they worked |
| **E2** | They told you what they did in the past | "Last Tuesday I had to call twice" |
| **E3** | They told you what they want or would do | "I would definitely use that" |
| **E4** | You believe it and have not checked it | A guess you are carrying |
| **E5** | You checked and it turned out wrong | Keep it visible, strike it through |

> **One rule has no bend in it.** Any answer to a would you question is E3. So is
> any answer to a do you think question, however many people gave it. Ten E3
> answers do not add up to one E1. No claim your case depends on may rest on E3.

## 1. What the starting document knows, and what it only believes
*0.75 page*

Every piece of work starts from something written down. A case brief, a strategy
memo, a ticket, a founder's one pager. Read it the way a lawyer reads a contract.
A few of its lines carry a number, a source, or a count. The rest is belief
wearing a suit, and your first job is to tell the two apart.

**Which claims arrive with proof attached? Which are simply stated, as if saying
them made them true? Which single belief, if it turned out to be wrong, would
sink the whole brief?**

Most starting documents carry six to ten claims worth listing. Two or three will
have a number behind them. The rest is where your week goes. Watch for the
sentence that explains why the problem exists. That one is almost always the
belief. It is also the one nobody thought to check.

## 4. Bets I wrote before I looked
*0.75 page*

Before you speak to anyone, write down what you expect to find. Each bet gets two
lines drawn in advance: the count that keeps it alive, and the count that kills
it. Write both as plain counts of your sample, such as six of nine, never as a
percent.

Set each kill line high enough that it can really trigger, because a bet that
cannot die was never a bet. Write at least four, and make one of them a bet you
privately expect to lose.

The gap between the two counts matters. Say a bet holds at six of nine and dies
at five of nine. You have left yourself no room to be unsure. Leave a real gap,
and treat any result that lands inside it as unclear rather than as a win.
Unclear is a finding, and it belongs in section seven with the rest.

> **Format only, from a domain your work will never touch.** I bet at least six of
> the nine mornings this week will start out cloudy. It holds at six of nine. It
> dies at three or fewer. After the week: it died, two of nine.

## 8. The step where it hurts most
*1.25 pages*

Lay the journey out as steps, then score every step, because a map where each
step matters equally says nothing. Score pain from one to five, score how often
it bites from one to five, and multiply the two.

**Your scores must leave one step standing alone at the top. If two steps tie,
add one more column of your own choosing. Break the tie, then say why you picked
that tie breaker.**

Do not agonise over whether something is a three or a four. The number exists to
force an order and to show your working to somebody who disagrees. It measures
nothing on its own. What matters is that the top step is clearly ahead, and that
you could defend the gap out loud.

One thing to watch for. The worst step is often the one where nothing is
happening from the company's side. Waiting, deciding, and worrying are steps, and
they stay invisible because no feature touches them.

## 9. Why it is still broken
*1 page*

If the fix were cheap and easy, someone would have shipped it by now. Ask why,
then ask why again, until you hit one of two walls: someone gains from things

## 10. The gaps, and why they are still open
*1.25 pages*

A gap is a hole your own evidence proves is there, plus a reason it is still open
right now. Write each one in the shape below, then put it through two tests. If
you could have written the page ten years ago, it fails. Then say what keeps the
gap open for the next three years.

The ten year test is stricter than it sounds. A problem that could have been
fixed ten years ago, and still is not fixed, is either too small to bother with.
Or something is blocking it. You have found neither. The thing that changed can
be a habit, a rule, a price, or a piece of technology. It cannot be that people
now have smartphones.

If a reader can guess the feature from reading your gap, rewrite it. A gap is the
hole. A feature is your answer to it, and that belongs in Part C.

> *[The group]* struggles to *[make the progress]* when *[the moment]*, because
> *[the blocker]*. It matters because *[the stakes]*.

| # | The gap, in the shape above | Why now, and not five years ago | What keeps it open for three more years |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | | | |
| 2 | | | |
| 3 | | | |

Tag and count every row. A gap with no tag is just an opinion.

> **So what?**
> *The gap I could not have written about ten years ago is [that one]. It is open
> now because [what changed].*

# Before you send it out

Read your paper once as your hardest reader would, with this list in hand. Every
unchecked box is a hole somebody else will find.

- [ ] Every section ends with a So what? line written in my own words.
- [ ] Every claim my case depends on carries an E1 or an E2 tag.
- [ ] No claim my case depends on rests on E3.
- [ ] My bets carry a date from before the fieldwork, with a holds count and a dies count.
- [ ] At least one bet died, and the count that killed it is on the page.
- [ ] Every promoted insight completes: I expected this, but found that.
- [ ] The coping work is counted on its own, with what it costs.
- [ ] I described how the system runs, including who pays and who gets paid on what number.
- [ ] My rings name people nobody pointed me at, including whoever can block a fix.
- [ ] I named the holes in my net before a reader could.
- [ ] My journey scores leave one step standing alone at the top.
- [ ] My why chain ends at a gain or a block, not at the app is bad.
- [ ] Every gap passes the ten year test.
- [ ] The runner up got a full argument, not a wave.
- [ ] My problem statement shuts out something I still believe in.
- [ ] I answered the strongest line in my own Part A that argues against my pick.
- [ ] My watch number is named, and it would really show harm.
- [ ] Both scenes are about the same person, and both read like scenes.
- [ ] Every part sits inside its page budget.

## The walk back test

Do this last, with a pen in your hand. Pick any one line from Part C and walk it
backwards, link by link: the line, the gap it serves, the surprise that fed the
gap, the thing you watched or heard, and the person it came from. If any link is
missing, the line is an ornament, and ornaments get cut.

> **Run the walk twice more, from the two lines you trust least. If all three
> walks end at a person, the paper is yours, and no one else could have written
> it.**
